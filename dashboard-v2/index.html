§ Research foundation

Option-Implied Event Contracts: A Derivatives Layer for Prediction Markets.

We introduce a family of derivatives priced under a Jacobi diffusion on the underlying probability. Closed-form Greeks, a variance-swap replication, and a belief-volatility index follow directly from the model. We show that the derivatives layer compresses cross-venue arbitrage bounds by O(100×) by shortening effective capital lockup from the event's resolution horizon to the derivative's expiry.

§ 05 · Terminal Pinning

How fast does belief saturate?

The information-driven bridge model says the variance Pt(1 − Pt) must collapse to zero as t → T at a rate set by a single parameter β0, calibrated from realized quadratic variation of the logit price. The empirical trajectory should sit beneath the theoretical envelope — the upper bound of Theorem 3.2.

§ Closed forms

The bridge gives you two things diffusion baselines can't.

Pinning rate. Under β(t) = β0/√(T−t), the variance has an explicit upper bound: 𝔼[Pt(1−Pt)] ≤ ((T−t)/T)β02/2 (Theorem 3.2). The Jacobi diffusion has no analog of this — its variance can in principle wander indefinitely.

ATM implied vol asymptote. Near resolution, the at-the-money OIEC implied vol approaches Σ*(p, p) = 2 Φ−1(1 − p/2) — a closed-form function of the spot probability only, with no model parameters (Theorem 4.2, Remark 4.4). At p = 0.5 this gives 1.349, regardless of σ̂ or τ.

About this framework

OIECs are derivatives written on prediction-market event futures. Under a Jacobi diffusion dP = σ√P(1−P) dW, the surface of calls and puts across strikes admits closed-form Greeks and a variance-swap replication that defines the Belief Volatility Index (BVIX): a VIX analogue for the probability itself.
